# An Uncommon, Statistically Rigorous Intraday Edge-Discovery & Validation Pipeline for MNQ/MES and FX — Research Report + Claude Code Specification

## TL;DR
- A genuine, defensible intraday edge on MNQ/MES/FX is almost certainly modest (out-of-sample Sharpe ~0.7–1.5, single-edge Calmar ~0.5–1.5); the trader's ~10%/month-at-4%-drawdown goal (a sustained annualized Calmar well above 2.5) is realistically attainable only by combining several uncorrelated weak-but-real edges, not from one miraculous signal — and most candidate edges will be killed by costs and overfitting before they ever trade.
- The correct path is a discovery-then-validation pipeline whose CORE is Timothy Masters' permutation-test suite (in-sample MCPT + walk-forward MCPT on bar-permuted OHLC) used as a hard rejection gate BEFORE spending scarce out-of-sample data, layered with Lopez de Prado's anti-overfitting machinery (triple-barrier/meta-labeling, purged k-fold + embargo, CPCV, Deflated Sharpe, PBO) and honest tick-accurate cost modeling.
- The "50K Flex Eval" (4% EOD-measured max loss, no daily loss limit, 50% consistency in eval only, 40-micro cap) is unusually friendly to a robust system: the EOD drawdown definition means intraday excursions don't breach, so the optimal eval plan is to size small, spread ~6% profit across ≥3 sessions to clear consistency, then re-frame the funded ~10%/month target honestly against measured expectancy.

## Key Findings

1. **Be honest about the ceiling.** Selection bias and non-normality systematically inflate backtest Sharpe; the deflated Sharpe ratio exists precisely because, as Bailey & López de Prado put it in *The Deflated Sharpe Ratio* (Journal of Portfolio Management 40(5), 2014), a backtest optimizer "has no problem finding a 'profitable' strategy with any desired Sharpe ratio. Yet when this 'profitable' strategy is applied to a second similar-length pseudorandom time series, it typically" collapses — and they warn that "just as a spring memorizes its equilibrium position," over-tensioned financial variables snap back violently. Realistic, survivable retail intraday Sharpe sits around 1; anything backtesting above ~2–3 is a red flag for overfitting or under-modeled costs.
2. **Validation must precede, and gate, discovery.** Masters' key insight: out-of-sample data is a consumable resource — every time you peek at it you burn statistical credibility. Permutation tests let you reject bad ideas using in-sample data + synthetic nulls, preserving OOS data for a final, rarely-used confirmation.
3. **The bar-permutation MCPT is the workhorse.** Shuffle log-returns (intrabar OHLC moves and overnight gaps permuted separately), rebuild synthetic OHLC, re-optimize the whole strategy on each of ~1,000 permutations, and compute a p-value = fraction of permutations beating the real result. Thresholds: p<0.01 in-sample; walk-forward p<0.05 on one year, p<0.01 on two+ years.
4. **Uncommon edges with the best statistical hygiene** are: information-driven bars + microstructure proxies (tick imbalance, signed volume, Kyle's lambda, Amihud, Roll), conditional intraday seasonality with multiple-testing control, ES–NQ lead-lag and cointegration/OU spread reversion, regime-gating via HMM/GARCH, and meta-labeling on top of a simple primary signal.
5. **Costs are the main killer.** MNQ tick = $0.50, MES tick = $1.25 (CME Group: "The Micro E-mini S&P 500 futures contract is $5 x the S&P 500 Index and has a minimum tick of 0.25 index points"); realistic all-in round-trip cost per micro ≈ 1 tick + commission. A scalp targeting 4 ticks that ignores a 1-tick cost overstates edge by ~25%+. Under-modeling slippage is the most common reason intraday edges evaporate.
6. **High Calmar comes from diversification.** Combining k uncorrelated equal-risk return streams cuts volatility by ~1/√k (4 streams halve risk; 9 cut it by two-thirds). This — not a single signal — is the realistic route to the trader's risk-adjusted target.

## Details

### 1. Setting honest expectations (read this first)
The trader wants ≥10%/month on ~$50k notional within a 4–5% max drawdown. On a funded account that is an annualized return of well over 100% at a Calmar ratio north of 2.5 sustained — territory occupied by top-decile professional CTAs only in their best years, and essentially never by a lone retail trader on one signal. The literature is blunt: Bailey and López de Prado show a backtest optimizer "usually has no problem finding a 'profitable' strategy with any desired Sharpe ratio," which then reverts violently out of sample. Practitioner consensus puts realistic retail intraday Sharpe near 1.0, with anything above ~2–3 a strong overfitting/under-costing warning.

What IS attainable: a *portfolio* of several genuine, low-correlation edges, each with OOS Sharpe ~0.7–1.2, sized conservatively, can plausibly produce a smoothed equity curve with Calmar ~1.5–2.5 and monthly returns that, on a leveraged prop account with the EOD-drawdown rule, *can* episodically reach 10% in good months while keeping closing-equity drawdown near 4%. The honest framing for the pipeline: target the ~6% needed to PASS the eval with high probability, then let measured expectancy — not a wished-for number — dictate funded sizing. Any process tuned to hit "10%" rather than to measure true expectancy is, by construction, a failure mode.

### 2. The validation core: Timothy Masters' permutation-test methodology
Masters' framework (from *Testing and Tuning Market Trading Systems* and *Permutation and Randomization Tests for Trading System Development*) rests on a sharp null hypothesis: **the strategy is worthless — its position decisions are unrelated to subsequent market moves.** This differs from (and usually beats) the bootstrap's null of zero mean return, because in a biased market (e.g. the S&P's secular drift) a model that is merely long-biased will fool a mean-zero test but not a permutation test. Masters proves that under the worthless-strategy null, the rank statistic is uniformly distributed, so the fraction of permutation results ≥ the real result is a valid (quasi) p-value.

**(a) Bar-level (not trade-level) objective functions.** Compute profit factor / Sharpe on per-bar strategy returns, not per-trade. Bar-level returns are far more numerous and statistically stable, give the permutation machinery the granular pairing it needs, and avoid the small-sample noise of a handful of trades. Masters recommends ≥50 (ideally ≥200) opportunities and ≥1,000 permutations.

**(b) In-sample Monte Carlo Permutation Test (MCPT).** The bar-permutation algorithm: (i) convert OHLC to log space; (ii) decompose each bar into *relative intrabar moves* (open-to-high, open-to-low, open-to-close as displacements from the prior close) and the *overnight gap* (prior close to current open) — these are permuted **separately** so the synthetic series preserves intrabar geometry and gap statistics; (iii) shuffle the time order of these relative components; (iv) reconstruct a synthetic OHLC path by cumulatively summing the shuffled log components and exponentiating. The synthetic series keeps the marginal return distribution, volatility, skew and kurtosis of the original but destroys trend, autocorrelation, regime cycles and any repeating structure. **Crucially, the entire strategy — including its parameter optimization — is re-run on each permutation**, so the resulting p-value measures data-mining bias, i.e. the probability a worthless strategy + the same search could match the real performance. Pass gate: **p < 0.01** in-sample.

**(c) Walk-forward testing.** Roll a train/test window forward: optimize parameters on an in-sample window, trade them un-touched on the next out-of-sample block, step forward, repeat, and stitch the OOS blocks into one continuous equity curve. This tests robustness to regime change and reoptimization, not a single curve fit. (Be aware of walk-forward's own leakage pitfall: indicators with long lookbacks reference prior in-sample bars at each split — more splits, more leakage.)

**(d) Walk-forward MCPT.** Permute only the OOS data *after the first training fold*, re-run the whole walk-forward (including reoptimization) on each permutation, and compute the fraction matching/beating the real walk-forward metric. This estimates the probability a worthless strategy could produce the observed walk-forward result. Thresholds (Masters/practitioner rule of thumb): **p < 0.05 for one year of OOS, p < 0.01 for two or more years.**

**Documented flaws to state plainly in report and code:** bar permutation destroys volatility clustering and long-memory (so it is *anti-conservative* for strategies that genuinely exploit volatility persistence, and conservative for others); it assumes non-overlapping trades; and it can be distorted by strongly asymmetric raw-return distributions. Mitigations: use block/stationary-bootstrap variants as a cross-check, center returns to neutralize long/short bias (Masters shows centering the raw returns exactly removes the bias component), and never rely on a single test.

**Why the ordering matters:** reusing OOS/validation data accumulates selection bias. The permutation tests are cheap synthetic-null screens that let you kill the great majority of candidates *before* touching real OOS data, so the final OOS confirmation remains statistically meaningful.

### 3. Anti-overfitting machinery (Lopez de Prado, Bailey)
- **Triple-barrier labeling:** label each event by which of three barriers is hit first — upper (profit-take), lower (stop), or vertical (time). This is path-dependent and respects stops, unlike fixed-horizon labels.
- **Meta-labeling:** keep a simple primary model for *direction* (side), then train a secondary ML model to decide *whether to act and how big* (a binary "take/skip" + size). This raises precision/F1 and is far more robust than asking ML to predict direction outright. Use it for filtering and sizing, not prediction.
- **Purged k-fold CV + embargo:** remove training observations whose label horizons overlap the test set (purge), then drop a further fraction after each test fold (embargo, e.g. 1–5%) to defeat leakage from serial correlation.
- **Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV):** generate many train/test combinations to produce a *distribution* of OOS Sharpes and backtest paths rather than one number, enabling statistical inference and direct PBO estimation.
- **Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO):** via combinatorially-symmetric cross-validation (CSCV), the probability that the in-sample-best configuration underperforms the median OOS. Report it; high PBO means your selection process — not the strategy — is the problem.
- **Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR):** the single most important multiple-testing correction. Precise formulae (Bailey & López de Prado 2014):

  Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio: PSR(SR\*) = Z[ ((SR̂ − SR\*)·√(n−1)) / √(1 − γ₃·SR̂ + ((γ₄−1)/4)·SR̂²) ], where Z is the standard-normal CDF, SR̂ the observed (non-annualized) Sharpe, n the number of return observations, γ₃ skewness, γ₄ (non-excess) kurtosis. Negative skew and high kurtosis inflate the denominator and lower PSR — penalizing non-normal returns.

  Expected maximum Sharpe (deflation benchmark): SR\*₀ = √(V[SR]) · [ (1−γ)·Z⁻¹(1 − 1/N) + γ·Z⁻¹(1 − 1/(N·e)) ], where γ ≈ 0.5772 (Euler–Mascheroni), N = number of independent trials, V[SR] = variance of the trials' Sharpes, Z⁻¹ the inverse normal CDF, e Euler's number.

  Deflated Sharpe: DSR = PSR(SR\*₀). **All inputs must be in non-annualized units** (a common implementation bug is mixing annualized SR with non-annualized moments). Decision rule: **DSR > 0.95**. SR\*₀ rises with both the number of trials and the dispersion of trial Sharpes — so honestly tracking the trial count N is mandatory. In Bailey & López de Prado's worked example, an annualized SR̂ of 2.5 with skew −3, kurtosis 10, T=1250, N=100 trials yields DSR ≈ 0.90 — *rejected* at the 95% bar; the same result after only 46 trials would have given 0.9505 and been accepted, illustrating how trial count alone decides significance.

  Minimum track record length: MinTRL = (1 − γ₃·SR̂ + ((γ₄−1)/4)·SR̂²)·(Z₁₋α/(SR̂ − SR\*))² observations — tells you whether you even have enough data to claim significance.

- Complementary corrections: Bonferroni / Benjamini–Hochberg FDR across the explicit family of seasonality/feature tests.
- **Python tooling:** mlfinlab (now commercial), the open `purgedcv`/`purged-cross-validation` and `skfolio` packages for CPCV/DSR, `pypbo` for PBO, `hmmlearn` for HMM, `arch` for GARCH/EGARCH/HAR, `statsmodels` for Engle-Granger/Johansen/VECM, `arbitragelab` for OU half-life.

### 4. Survey of uncommon edge-discovery approaches

**4.1 Microstructure & order-flow proxies (from bar/tick data, no L2 feed).** Build Lopez de Prado information-driven bars — tick, volume, dollar, and *imbalance/run* bars — which sample on activity rather than clock time and have markedly lower serial correlation and more Gaussian returns (better statistical behaviour for everything downstream). From these compute: tick-rule trade signs and signed volume; tick/volume imbalance; VPIN (volume-synchronized probability of informed trading) = Σ|V_buy − V_sell| / (n·V) over volume buckets, which tends to precede volatility; Kyle's λ (price impact per unit signed volume, via regression of returns on signed volume); Amihud illiquidity λᴬ = mean(|r|/dollar-volume); and Roll's effective spread from the autocovariance of price changes (2√(−cov(Δp_t, Δp_{t−1}))). All five (Roll 1984, Corwin-Schultz, Kyle 1985, Amihud 2002, Hasbrouck) are derivable from OHLCV bars alone, though signed-volume estimators are more accurate with a true tick feed. Applicability: MNQ/MES have rich CME volume; FX "tick volume" is a proxy only. These are best used as *conditioning/meta-labeling features*, not standalone signals.

**4.2 Conditional intraday seasonality.** Index futures exhibit robust intraday structure: the U-shaped volume/volatility curve (Andersen–Bollerslev), open-auction momentum (the opening-range return predicting day direction has documented predictive power even after costs in index futures, per Taiwan-futures and related studies), lunch lull, close-auction and MOC imbalance effects, overnight-to-RTH gap behavior, day-of-week and month-end rebalancing, and options-expiry / quad-witching dynamics. *Test rigorously with multiple-comparison control* (ANOVA/Kruskal-Wallis across buckets — the standard ANOVA + Tukey HSD + Kruskal-Wallis approach used in day-of-week overnight-return studies — then Benjamini-Hochberg) because the space of "time-of-day × day-of-week × event" buckets is large and false positives are guaranteed if uncorrected. Bailey et al. explicitly use a seasonal strategy as their canonical overfitting example — so seasonality findings MUST pass MCPT + DSR.

**4.3 Cross-market lead-lag & statistical arbitrage.** The E-mini complex shows measurable price-discovery structure: Hasbrouck (2003, *Intraday Price Formation in U.S. Equity Index Markets*) finds the E-mini futures dominate roughly 90% of S&P 500 price discovery among the ETF, floor-traded futures and E-mini, with similar dominance for the Nasdaq-100; in FX, regular EUR/USD futures account for on the order of two-thirds of price discovery in the EUR/USD market via Hasbrouck/Gonzalo-Granger information shares (CME EUR/USD studies on intraday data). Cross-market cash-vs-futures activity peaks at the speed-of-light propagation delay — the Dobrev-Schaumburg estimator detects a sharp peak at ~5 ms one-way, consistent with fiber propagation between the futures match engine in Aurora, IL and the cash venue in Secaucus, NJ (note CME's Aurora engines sit ~40 miles west of Chicago; the NY↔Aurora fiber round-trip is ~13–25 ms, with microwave links cutting it to ~8 ms). At millisecond scale this is unexploitable for retail, but at the 1-minute scale **conditioning** signals survive: ES↔NQ relative strength, bond/equity (ZN vs ES) risk-on/off, DXY↔FX, gold↔real-yields, and futures-vs-cash basis. Trade the *spread* with cointegration: Engle-Granger or Johansen to find the hedge ratio, model the residual as Ornstein-Uhlenbeck dy = θ(μ−y)dt + σdW, estimate the **half-life of mean reversion = ln(2)/θ** (via the AR(1) coefficient on the spread) to set holding horizon and entry/exit z-score bands. Verify spread stationarity (ADF) and that half-life is short enough for intraday (minutes-to-hours). MES–MNQ is itself a tradeable, capital-efficient micro spread within the 40-micro cap.

**4.4 Regime detection & conditioning.** Generalize the transcripts' "cluster by RSI" idea: fit a Gaussian HMM (`hmmlearn`) or GMM/k-means on volatility + trend + microstructure features to label latent regimes (calm/trending/stressed), and trade a simple edge ONLY in the regime where it works. HMM outputs posterior probabilities (e.g. 0.22 calm / 0.78 stressed) that can scale risk smoothly rather than switch all-or-nothing; published work shows using an HMM to avoid trading in high-volatility regimes can eliminate losing trades and lift Sharpe. Gate sizing with volatility forecasts: GARCH/EGARCH for clustering and asymmetry, and HAR-RV (realized-volatility heterogeneous autoregression) for multi-horizon forecasting. Caveat: HMMs can overfit and "flicker"; constrain state count (2–3), use sticky transition priors, and validate regime assignment out of sample.

**4.5 Rigorous ML for intraday signals.** Pipeline: information-driven bars → triple-barrier labels → meta-labeling → purged k-fold/CPCV → MDA & SHAP feature importance → DSR/PBO. Prefer interpretable, low-parameter models (shallow gradient-boosted trees, or logistic meta-labels) over deep nets. Emphatically: meta-label on top of a transparent primary signal; do not ask a black box to predict raw direction. (López de Prado's "10 reasons ML funds fail" centers on exactly the leakage/overfitting traps this pipeline guards against.)

**4.6 Volatility/options-implied conditioning.** VIX level and term-structure slope (contango vs backwardation), VVIX, put/call, and dealer **gamma exposure (GEX)** regime are useful *conditioning variables*: positive-GEX / above-gamma-flip environments are empirically mean-reverting and vol-suppressed (favor fade/range tactics), negative-GEX environments are trending/explosive (favor momentum), as documented by SpotGamma/FlashAlpha practitioner research. Caveat: GEX figures from third-party vendors are estimates with modeling assumptions; treat as a soft regime gate, validate, and never as a precise level.

**4.7 Path-dependent / sequence patterns.** Replace discretionary ICT labels with *systematic pattern mining*: enumerate conditional probabilities of continuation after specific quantified intraday structures (e.g. defined gap+drive sequences, n-bar momentum-then-pause), then validate each by permutation. This converts "I see a pattern" into "this conditional edge survives a worthless-strategy null."

**4.8 Ensemble / portfolio-of-edges.** This is the load-bearing finding. k uncorrelated equal-risk/equal-return streams reduce portfolio volatility by ~1/√k (4 → halve risk, 9 → cut by two-thirds, 16 → −75%), and negatively-correlated streams do better still. Managed-futures research bears this out: Simplify Asset Management finds (using the SG CTA Index) that "even a 10% allocation to CTAs produces immediate value," with risk-adjusted gains peaking before allocations exceed 30–40%, and their four-year study (Mar 2022–Feb 2026) shows a 60/30/10 stock/bond/CTA mix delivered "more return, less volatility, and a shallower maximum drawdown" than a traditional 60/40. For this trader, the realistic route to a high Calmar that respects the EOD drawdown and the 50% consistency rule is to run 3–6 low-correlation intraday edges (e.g. an open-auction momentum edge, a midday mean-reversion edge, an ES–NQ spread edge, a regime-gated overnight-gap edge), each sized small, combined with correlation-aware weighting (inverse-variance or simple risk parity). Diversification across uncorrelated signals is usually the ONLY realistic path to high Calmar.

### 5. Cost & power modeling (do this or the whole exercise is theatre)
- **MNQ:** $2 multiplier, tick 0.25 = **$0.50/tick**. **MES:** $5 multiplier, tick 0.25 = **$1.25/tick** (CME Group product spec). Commissions roughly $0.25–$1.00 round-trip per micro at discount futures brokers. Model **at least 1 tick of slippage + commission per round trip per contract**, and stress with 2 ticks. A 4-tick MNQ scalp ($2.00 gross) facing 1 tick ($0.50) + ~$0.50 commission keeps only ~$1.00 — a 50% haircut. This is why high-frequency scalps usually die.
- **FX (MetaTrader):** model spread + commission + swap. EUR/USD raw spread ~0.1 pip + ~$3.50/lot/side commission (≈0.7–0.9 pip all-in on a standard lot at typical retail, e.g. measured averages of ~0.91 pip on mark-up accounts); XAU/USD spread ~1–2.5 pips normal, blowing out to 50+ pips around NFP/FOMC; add 3–10 pips slippage on gold in news. Swap only matters if held overnight (day trades close before rollover).
- **Power:** require ≥200 trades (Masters' "desirable" sample) per edge before believing a Sharpe; compute MinTRL to confirm the track record is long enough; never trust an edge built on <50 events.
- **Interaction with eval rules:** because cost-per-trade is fixed in dollars, lower-frequency edges amortize costs better — favor edges with average holds of minutes-to-hours over sub-minute scalps. As one practitioner survey of 2024–2026 results put it, at aggregate costs around 0.4% "the alpha of standard strategies statistically vanishes" unless a strategy trades infrequently enough to amortize cost or earns rebates.

### 6. Engineering the trade plan to the 50K Flex Eval
Exact rules to design around: $50,000 account; **$3,000 (6%) profit target**; **$2,000 (4%) max loss measured on END-OF-DAY closing equity**; **no daily loss limit**; **50% consistency rule in evaluation only** (no single day > 50% of cumulative profit); **max 4 minis / 40 micros**; no consistency rule once funded.

Implications the pipeline must exploit:
- **EOD drawdown is a gift.** Because the trail/floor is measured on closing equity, intraday excursions below the threshold do NOT breach as long as you close the day above the floor. This permits wider intraday stops / mean-reversion holds than an intraday-trailing firm would allow — but it is NOT a license to martingale; the code must still cap intraday risk so a gap can't blow the EOD number. (Note: MFF-style Flex variants typically lock the floor at start+$100 once EOD equity reaches it; some Flex products instead use a *fixed* 4% floor that does not trail. The spec instructs Claude Code to parameterize the exact floor/lock mechanic and confirm against the trader's contract.)
- **Consistency rule forces diversification of P&L across days.** To clear $3,000 with no day >50% of total, you need profit spread across ≥3 meaningful days (e.g. target ~$1,000–$1,400/day for 3–4 days). Practitioner accounts confirm a single $1,500+ day on a $3,000 target pushes the ratio past 50% and forces extra days. This dovetails perfectly with the portfolio-of-edges approach and argues *against* swinging for one home-run session.
- **Sizing:** within 40 micros, size each edge so that a worst-plausible-day (modeled from the OOS drawdown distribution, not the average) keeps closing equity above the floor with margin. Target ~6% to pass with high probability; do not over-size to chase 10% during the eval.
- **Funded phase:** drop consistency; let the measured portfolio expectancy and the EOD floor define sizing. Frame the ~10%/month goal as a *good-month outcome of a system whose expected month is lower*, with explicit drawdown probabilities from the validated equity-curve simulations.

## Recommendations (staged, with go/no-go thresholds)

**Stage 0 — Infrastructure (week 1).** Have Claude Code ingest/clean futures + FX data, align to exchange time (CME Central / handle DST), stitch continuous contracts with roll adjustment, build time bars AND dollar/imbalance bars, and stand up the cost model. Benchmark: a reproducible, leak-checked data layer with unit tests.

**Stage 1 — Candidate generation (weeks 2–3).** Engineer the feature library (microstructure, seasonality, intermarket, regime, path). Generate candidate edges, *logging every trial* for the DSR trial count N. Benchmark to proceed: a candidate must show in-sample bar-level profit factor > ~1.1 *after costs* AND a stable parameter plateau (not a lone spike).

**Stage 2 — Permutation gating (weeks 3–4).** Run in-sample MCPT (≥1,000 perms). **Reject unless p < 0.01.** Survivors go to walk-forward, then walk-forward MCPT: **reject unless p < 0.05 (1yr) / p < 0.01 (2yr+).** Only now compute DSR (require **>0.95**) and PBO (require low, e.g. <0.2). This ordering preserves OOS data.

**Stage 3 — Portfolio assembly (week 5).** Take the surviving uncorrelated edges (pairwise |ρ| ideally <0.3), weight by inverse variance / risk parity, and simulate the combined equity curve through the EOD-drawdown and consistency-rule logic. Go-live-on-sim threshold: combined OOS Calmar > ~1.5 and modeled probability of passing the eval > ~80% at ≤40 micros.

**Stage 4 — Forward paper/sim (weeks 6–10).** Trade the portfolio on sim/demo to confirm execution fills match modeled costs. Kill any edge whose live fills degrade Sharpe by >~30% vs model. Only then attempt the paid eval.

**What changes the plan:** if no edge survives Stage 2, do NOT loosen thresholds — that is the overfitting trap; instead broaden the *hypothesis* space (new data, new conditioning) or accept that no edge was found. If DSR < 0.95 purely because N (trial count) ballooned, the honest fix is fewer, better-motivated hypotheses, not more searching.

## Caveats
- Permutation tests have real, named weaknesses (destroy volatility clustering and long memory; assume non-overlapping trades); they are necessary, not sufficient — cross-check with block bootstrap and forward sim.
- Backtest overfitting is essentially unavoidable with enough trials; the DSR/PBO machinery *bounds* it but cannot eliminate it. A passing in-sample result is permission to spend OOS data, not proof of profit.
- GEX/options-positioning and FX "tick volume" are vendor estimates/proxies with modeling assumptions; use as soft regime gates only.
- Prop-firm rules change and vary by variant; the exact EOD-floor/lock mechanic, consistency math, and micro cap must be confirmed against the trader's specific contract before sizing.
- Most importantly: genuine edges are modest and perishable. The system must monitor live performance against the validated equity bands and stand down a degrading edge. Any result that looks too good is, until proven otherwise, a bug or an under-modeled cost.
